Structured Data: Making Content Machine-Readable

Structured Data uses Schema.org markup (usually JSON-LD) to explicitly tell search engines and now especially AI systems, what your content is about, who wrote it, and how it should be understood. Traffic Torch weights this at 15% because properly implemented structured data dramatically increases the chances of rich results, AI citations, and accurate summarization.

What is Structured Data?

Structured Data is standardized code (most commonly JSON-LD) added to your page that labels elements like articles, FAQs, how-to guides, authors, dates, reviews, and more using the Schema.org vocabulary.

Launched in 2011 by Google, Bing, Yahoo, and Yandex through the Schema.org initiative, it was originally designed for rich snippets (stars, prices, event dates). By 2023‚Äì2026, it became one of the most powerful signals for AI search engines to correctly parse, trust, and quote content, especially for FAQPage, HowTo, Article, and Person types.

Official reference: Schema.org ‚Äì the vocabulary behind structured data

EEAT Signals: Demonstrating Expertise, Experience, Authoritativeness & Trust

EEAT (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness) is Google's framework for evaluating content quality. It's now a core ranking and AI-citation factor in 2026. Traffic Torch weights EEAT at 15% because pages with visible author signals, fresh dates, trusted outbound links, and HTTPS are far more likely to be cited by AI systems and trusted by users.

What are EEAT Signals?

EEAT is the updated version of Google's original E-A-T (Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness) concept, with "Experience" added in December 2022. It guides how Google (and now AI overviews) assess whether content is produced by someone who has real-world experience with the topic, demonstrates expertise, is authoritative in the field, and can be trusted.

EEAT became especially important after the Helpful Content Update (2022‚Äì2023) and the rise of generative AI search. Pages lacking clear EEAT signals are heavily demoted or ignored in AI-generated answers. Even if they rank well for traditional blue links.

Scannability measures how easily users (and AI crawlers) can understand your content structure at a glance, through headings, lists, tables, and short paragraphs. What is Scannability?

Scannability refers to how well a page is structured to allow quick comprehension without requiring full linear reading. It relies on visual hierarchy (headings), chunking (lists, bullets), data presentation (tables), and bite-sized paragraphs.

This concept has been a core UX and SEO principle since Google's Mobilegeddon (2015) and accelerated with Core Web Vitals (2021) and AI-driven search. In the AI era, scannable pages are far more likely to have key information correctly pulled into overviews - because both humans and models prefer clear, hierarchical content.

Readability evaluates how easy your text is to read and comprehend, through sentence length, word choice, passive voice avoidance, and rhythm variation. What is Readability?

Readability measures how accessible your writing is to the average reader. Focusing on clear sentence structure, familiar words, active voice, and natural flow.

The concept dates back to the 1940s with formulas like Flesch-Kincaid and Gunning Fog, but became a major SEO factor after Google's Helpful Content Update (2022+) and the shift toward people-first content. What is Anti-AI Safety?

Anti-AI Safety evaluates whether your writing avoids the statistical fingerprints left by large language models, such as overused transition words, identical sentence openers, unnatural repetition of rare terms, or overly uniform sentence lengths.
